Annual: a plant completing its whole life cycle, from seed to flowering and fruiting, within the same year.

Anther: the male, pollen-bearing part of the flower.

Basal: referring to leaves forming from the base of the plant.

Biennial: a plant which completes its life cycle within two years, usually forming leafy rosettes in the first year then flowers, fruits and dies in the second year.

Bract: modified or specialised leaf usually situated at the base of a flower.

Bulbil: small bulb formed in the place of a flower or leaf axil which breaks off to form a new plant.

Calcareous: soil formed on calcium carbonate rich rocks such as limestone or chalk.

Calyx: the collective term for the sepals. Typically forming a protective layer around a flower in bud.

Carpel: the female reproductive organ of a flower.

Catkin: a spike of tiny flowers, usually either male or female and often drooping.

Cluster: a loose group of flowers.

Composite: a collective name used to describe members of the Daisy family.

Corolla: collective term for the petals of a flower.

Cushion: a plant forming dense rounded tufts of often numerous, tight leaf rosettes.

Filament: the stalk of a stamen.

Floret: the individual, often small, flowers clustered up within a flowerhead.

Flowerhead: refers to an often tightly grouped cluster of flowers, typically at the tip of a stem.

Fringe: refers to the cut margin of a petal.

Glandular hair: hair tipped with a gland, seen as a swelling or blob.

Hip: the fruit of a rose.

Hood: referring to the upper petals of certain flowers forming a 'hood-like' cap.

Inflorescence: a group or cluster of flowers arranged as a structural unit.

Keel: the lowest petal of a flower of the Pea family formed by the fusion of two petals and having a prominent 'keel-like' ridge along the join.

Lance-shaped: a spear-shaped leaf, broadly oval and tapering to a point.

Midrib: the central and often most prominent vein of a leaf.

Net-veined: leaf veins forming an interlacing network.

Palmate: leaves lobed and spreading outwards resembling fingers pointing away from the palm.

Perennial: any plant living for more than two years, sometimes woody and flowering each year.

Petal: the innermost floral leaves of a flower, usually brightly coloured.

Pinnate: a leaf consisting of opposite pairs of leaflets along a central axis, with or without an end leaflet.

Pinnately-lobed: a leaf with several pairs of shallow or deep lobes, but not distinct leaflets.

Rosette: leaves arranged in a cluster, usually at the base of the stem, and radiating outwards.

Runner: a slender, trailing stem producing roots and sometimes erect shoots at the nodes.

Scale leaf: a reduced leaf, green, brown or colourless, occurring on the stem.

Sepal: the outer ring of floral leaves of a flower, usually green but sometimes large, colourful and conspicuous.

Species: a population of individuals sharing common features and ancestry and capable of interbreeding producing fertile offspring.

Spike: a dense, elongated group of unstalked flowers, often pointed.

Spur: a conical or tubular extension to a petal often containing nectar.

Stamen: the male organ of a flower.

Stigma: the pollen-receiving tip of a style.

Stipule: a leaf-like appendage at the base of a leaf-stalk.

Style: an elongated part of the carpel projecting from the ovary to the stigma.

Tendril: a slender, twisting filament forming part of a leaf or stem enabling the plant to climb.

Tepal: describes sepals and petals if they look alike.

Toothed: regularly incised serrations along a leaf margin.

Trifoliate: leaves with three distinct leaflets.

Tufted: growing in dense clumps with short branches all arising at the same level.

Two-lipped: referring to a corolla which is divided in a way that resemble two lips of an open mouth.

Umbel: a flower cluster in which all stalks arise from a central point to give a flat-topped inflorescence.

Whorl: a ring of leaves or flowers arising from a central point on a stem.

Winged: a thin flange of tissue extending beyond the normal outline of the stem.
